Hawaii's Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) establishes a minimum amount of renewable energy that must be sold by the state's electricity providers. This page identifies and describes the features of the RPS and how they work together to define the minimum amount of renewable energy that will be sold in Hawaii.

{% endblock %} {% block state_overview %}
  • 30% of the state's energy consumption must be from renewable sources by 2020
  • Carve-outs for distributed generation
  • Explicit cost cap at 2 percent of electric bill annually
  • Two separate state RPS schedules (primary and secondary)

    Colorado's RPS has a technology minimum of 3% of the total RPS requirement by 2020, ramping up from a starting requirement of 1%.

    {% endblock %} {% block state_costcap %}

    Electric Resource Standards in Senate Bill 13-252 specify the cost of using renewable sources cannot exceed 2% of customer's electricity bill annually. This rate impact rule applies to both IOUs and coops, and requires the RPS energy obligations to be reduced to the amount necessary to comply with the rule's limitation.

    {% endblock %} {% block state_statute %}

    Colorado is the first state to create a RPS through ballot initiative. In 2004, voters approved Amendment 37 requiring utilities serving 40,000 or more customers to generate or purchase enough renewable energy to comprise 10% of retail electric sales. It would be increased and modified two subsequent times to its current 20%. In 2013, a distributed generation carve-out was added, and coal methane gas and municipal solid waste became eligible technologies (subject to Public Utilities Commission's approval). The same bill--S.B. 252--created a separate RPS schedule for coops, requiring 20% of sales from renewables by 2020 and thereafter.
